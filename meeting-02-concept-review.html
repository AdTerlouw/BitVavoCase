14:05 Pinar — Product Manager

Alright, I've gone through all three directions and I have to say—you've done a solid job visualizing distinct approaches to the same problem. Let me give you my structured feedback.

Scenario 1: Traditional Update

Three positives:

  1. The goal-first framing ("What's your goal?") is smart. Instead of asking users to pick order types, you're asking about intent. That's a meaningful shift from our current approach where 60% of users default to simple orders because they don't understand the alternatives. Starting with intent could help bridge that gap.
  2. The risk indicator with the visual bar is clear and honest. Medium risk, two out of four bars—users immediately understand this isn't a "safe" button they're pressing. That's responsible design given our regulatory context.
  3. The step-by-step "how it works" breakdown is confidence-building. Three numbered steps demystify the process. It's exactly what our less experienced users need before committing.

Main concern:
The flow is long. Goal selection → explanation → order form → preview. That's four screens minimum before a user places an order. Our power users—the ones who already understand limit orders—might find this patronizing. Have we thought about a "skip to order" shortcut for returning users? We risk adding friction for the users who don't need education.

14:12 Pinar — Product Manager

Scenario 2: Simplification

Three positives:

  1. The coffee metaphor is genuinely clever. "Think of it like buying coffee"—that's accessible, relatable, and strips away the intimidation factor of financial jargon. It meets users where they are, especially beginners who might feel crypto is "not for them."
  2. The scenario tables showing three possible outcomes are excellent for expectation-setting. Users see exactly what could happen—price drops (you win), price never reaches (you miss out), price fluctuates (mixed feelings). This is the kind of transparency that builds trust, and it addresses our core tension around risk communication.
  3. The quiz before trading is a bold, trust-building move. It's a soft gate that ensures users actually understand what they're about to do. From a compliance perspective, this is gold. We could even track quiz completion rates against order success/failure rates.

Main concern:
The quiz is also my biggest hesitation. It could feel like homework or a barrier, especially for users who just want to act quickly. What's the fallback if someone fails the quiz? Do we block them? That could feel punitive rather than educational. I'd want to see data on how users react to mandatory checkpoints in trading flows—my gut says there's a segment who'll bounce.

14:18 Pinar — Product Manager

Scenario 3: Simulation Push

Three positives:

  1. The proactive prompt is a smart engagement mechanic. "Hi Ad, there's more to buying than you know!"—personalized, contextual, and meets users at a moment when they're already engaged (they're on the asset page). This addresses our discovery problem head-on.
  2. The simulation mode with real data but no real money is a game-changer. This directly solves the "fear of advanced features" problem. Users can experience a limit order executing without risking anything. The psychological safety here is huge for adoption.
  3. The interactive drag-to-set-price on the chart is delightful. It's tactile, visual, and immediately understandable. The user feels what they're doing. That's the kind of embodied learning that sticks.

Main concern:
Timing and frequency of the bottom sheet prompt. If this pops up every time a user views an asset, it'll become annoying fast. We need smart triggers—first-time users, users who've only ever done market orders, users who hover on "advanced" options. Also, what happens after they run a simulation? Does it track their simulated orders? Can they see a history? The learning loop needs to be clear.

14:24 Pinar — Product Manager

Biggest Opportunity & Biggest Risk

Biggest opportunity: Scenario 3 (Simulation Push).
This is the most innovative of the three. It tackles both discovery and education in a way that feels native to the product rather than bolted on. If we nail the triggers and the post-simulation journey, this could meaningfully move adoption of advanced features while maintaining trust. It's also differentiated—I don't see competitors doing this well.

Biggest risk: Also Scenario 3.
The simulation feature is the most complex to build and the hardest to get right. If the simulation doesn't feel "real enough" (laggy updates, unrealistic price movements), it'll undermine trust rather than build it. And if users get comfortable winning in simulation mode but then lose money in real trading, we'll have a perception problem. We need to be really clear about the difference between simulation and reality.

14:39 Dave — Lead Developer

Scenario 3 Feedback

Three positives:

  1. The drag interaction on the chart is the kind of front-end challenge I enjoy. It's achievable with proper touch handling and SVG manipulation.
  2. The animated result page with price dropping is a nice touch. Using requestAnimationFrame for the animation is the right call—I saw that in the prototype.
  3. The modular approach—simulation mode as a distinct "mode" within the trading flow—is architecturally clean if we design it well. It could be reusable across other order types.

Main concern:
Real-time data. The prototype shows static behavior, but in production, if we're saying "using real data," we need live price feeds for the simulation. That's either WebSocket infrastructure or polling. Either way, it's a dependency on our market data layer. I'd want to scope that carefully before committing.
