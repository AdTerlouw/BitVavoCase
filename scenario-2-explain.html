Limit Order

Wait for your price

Let's explain this with something familiar

☕

Think of it like buying coffee

Imagine you want a latte, but you think the current price of $5.50 is too high. You'd only buy it if it drops to $4.50.

With a limit order, you're telling the coffee shop:

"Buy me a latte at $4.50 or cheaper."

The coffee shop writes down your request. Whenever the price drops to $4.50, they'll automatically get you that latte. If the price never drops that low? You simply don't buy—and you don't lose anything.

Medium risk level

Your order may not execute if the price never reaches your target

What can happen?

Here are three possible outcomes:

1

Price drops to your target

✅
Time Market Price What Happens
9:00 AM $5.50 You place limit order at $4.50
11:30 AM $4.50 Coffee shop runs a flash sale
11:31 AM $4.50 Order executes — you get your latte for $4.50

Result: You saved $1.00 compared to the original price.

2

Price never reaches target

⏳
Time Market Price What Happens
9:00 AM $5.50 You place limit order at $4.50
12:00 PM $5.25 Price drops slightly but not enough
6:00 PM $5.75 Price goes back up — order stays open

Result: You didn't buy anything. You lost nothing, but you also didn't get the latte.

3

Price fluctuates around target

📈
Time Market Price What Happens
9:00 AM $5.50 You place limit order at $4.50
10:00 AM $4.50 Order executes — you get your latte!
2:00 PM $4.00 Price drops even lower after your purchase

Result: You got your latte at $4.50, but could have paid $0.50 less if you waited. Still better than $5.50!

The bottom line

A limit order helps you buy at a price you're comfortable with. You might save money, or you might miss out—but you're always in control of the maximum you'll pay.
